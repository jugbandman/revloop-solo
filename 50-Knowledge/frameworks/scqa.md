# SCQA: Situation, Complication, Question, Answer

A structure for any business case, proposal, or executive communication. Forces buyer-first framing and brevity.

## The Structure

### S — Situation
Orient the reader quickly. One paragraph max. What's the current state?

**Good:** "Your enterprise sales team closed $12M last year across 45 deals, with an average cycle of 90 days."

**Bad:** Starting with your company's history or a long market overview.

**Rule:** The reader should nod and think "yes, that's right" within 10 seconds.

### C — Complication
What's going wrong? Lead with cost, not symptoms.

**Three elements:**
1. **The cost** — quantified financial or operational impact
2. **Who's affected** — specific teams, roles, or customers
3. **Why it's getting worse** — the structural reason this won't fix itself

**Good:** "But 40% of deals are stalling after first demo, costing an estimated $8M in lost pipeline. The problem is accelerating because your new product line requires multi-stakeholder selling that your team hasn't been trained for."

**Bad:** "There are some challenges with the current approach" or listing symptoms without cost.

### Q — Question
Frame the single question from the buyer's perspective.

**Good:** "How can we reduce deal stall rates and capture the $8M in stuck pipeline before Q4 planning locks in next year's targets?"

**Bad:** "Should you buy our product?" or multiple questions that dilute focus.

**Rule:** This should be the question the economic buyer is already asking themselves (or should be).

### A — Answer
The recommended approach. Vendor-agnostic first, then position your solution.

**Two-part structure:**
1. "The approach that works is..." (agnostic of any specific vendor)
2. "We were found to meet these requirements because..." (position your solution)

**Why vendor-agnostic first:** The champion needs to sell the approach internally before they sell your product. Give them a narrative that works without your logo on it.

## When to Use SCQA

| Document | How SCQA Applies |
|----------|-----------------|
| Business case | The entire document follows SCQA |
| Executive summary | Compress SCQA into 4-5 sentences |
| Proposal | Problem section = S+C, solution section = Q+A |
| Follow-up email | S: "Great conversation about..." C: "The gap you described..." Q: implied, A: "Here's what I'd suggest as a next step" |
| Deal review | S: deal state, C: what's blocking, Q: what must happen, A: next actions |

## Common Mistakes

1. **Skipping the Complication cost.** "There's a problem" is not a complication. "$8M in stuck pipeline" is.
2. **Starting with the Answer.** Executives tune out if you pitch before they agree on the problem.
3. **Multiple Questions.** One clear question. If you have three, pick the one tied to the most money.
4. **Vendor-heavy Answer.** If your champion can't present the Answer section without mentioning your company name, it's too vendor-centric.
